## 🐟 Seasonal Sushi Guide — Late September

Late September is excellent for sushi. Here's what's in season:

| Fish | Japanese | Season Peak | Notes |
|------|----------|-------------|-------|
| **Sanma** (Pacific saury) | 秋刀魚 | Sep-Oct ⭐ | THE autumn fish. Rich, oily, grilled or as sashimi. A must-try |
| **Katsuo** (Bonito) | 鰹 | Sep-Oct (modori-gatsuo) | "Returning bonito" — fattier than spring bonito. Tataki-style |
| **Saba** (Mackerel) | 鯖 | Sep-Nov | Getting fatty for winter. Excellent as shimesaba (vinegared) |
| **Aji** (Horse mackerel) | 鰺 | Jun-Sep | End of peak but still excellent. Light, clean |
| **Shinko/Kohada** (Gizzard shad) | 新子/小鰭 | Sep-Oct | Classic Edomae sushi topping. The mark of a skilled chef |
| **Ikura** (Salmon roe) | いくら | Sep-Nov ⭐ | Fresh ikura season begins! Bright, popping, incredible |
| **Uni** (Sea urchin) | 雲丹 | Jun-Sep | End of Hokkaido bafun-uni season. Still creamy and rich |
| **Sake** (Salmon) | 鮭 | Sep-Nov | Wild autumn salmon. Different from farmed — earthier |

**Tip:** At any omakase, the chef will naturally feature what's in season. Mention you're interested in seasonal specialties — they love sharing knowledge.

## 🏪 Konbini (Convenience Store) Food Guide

Japanese convenience stores (7-Eleven, Lawson, FamilyMart) are a food category of their own. Don't skip this.

### Must-Try Items

| Item | Where | Price | Why |
|------|-------|-------|-----|
| **Onigiri** (rice balls) | All | ¥120-200 | Salmon (sake), tuna mayo, mentaiko (spicy cod roe), kombu. Learn the 3-step nori wrapper pull technique |
| **Tamago sando** (egg sandwich) | Lawson is best | ¥200-300 | Impossibly fluffy, sweet egg salad on crustless bread. Life-changing konbini food |
| **Famichiki** (fried chicken) | FamilyMart | ¥200 | Hot, juicy, crispy. At the counter. 7-Eleven's nanachiki and Lawson's L-Chiki are comparable |
| **Nikuman** (steamed meat buns) | All (hot case) | ¥150-200 | Fluffy, warming, satisfying. Pizza-man is the cheese version |
| **Premium onigiri** | 7-Eleven, Lawson | ¥200-300 | Higher-quality fish, worth the extra ¥50-100 |
| **Melon pan** | All | ¥130-200 | Sweet, crispy-topped bread. A Japanese classic |
| **7-Eleven coffee** | 7-Eleven | ¥110 | Machine-drip but genuinely excellent |
| **Oden** (late Sept may have it) | All | ¥80-150/piece | Fish cakes, daikon, egg in dashi broth. Point and choose |
| **Strong Zero** | All | ¥150 | 9% ABV chuhai. Exercise caution |
| **Haagen-Dazs Japan exclusives** | All | ¥300-400 | Flavors you can't get anywhere else |

### Pro Tips
- Visit late evening for markdowns on expiring items (still perfectly fresh)
- Konbini are open 24/7 — perfect for jet-lag midnight snacks
- You can heat purchases in the store microwave
- Konbini toilets are clean and free — lifesaver when exploring

## 🏬 Depachika (Department Store Food Hall) Guide

The basement floors of Japanese department stores contain stunning food halls — one of Tokyo's best food experiences.

### Best Depachika

| Store | Area | Highlights |
|-------|------|-----------|
| **Isetan Shinjuku B1-B2** | Shinjuku-sanchome | The king of depachika. Insane variety. Don't miss wagashi section and prepared meal counters. Great for assembling a hotel room picnic |
| **Mitsukoshi Ginza B2-B3** | Ginza | Traditional Japanese sweets, high-end bento, premium prepared foods |
| **Tokyu Food Show** | Shibuya (under station) | More modern, approachable. Good bento selection |
| **Daimaru Tokyo B1** | Tokyo Station | Perfect for grabbing ekiben or sweets before a train journey |

### Tips
- Visit **30-60 min before closing** for "hangaku" (half-price) stickers. Quality stays high, price drops dramatically
- Free samples are common — try before you buy
- Wagashi (traditional sweets) make excellent souvenirs
- Assemble a depachika bento picnic for a budget-luxury meal in your hotel
